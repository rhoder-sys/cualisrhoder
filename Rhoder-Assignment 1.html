Fairfax County Public Library System

System Introduction

Context:

First established in 1939 by the Fairfax County Board of Supervisors, the Fairfax County Public Library System consists of 23 library branches, eight of which are regional libraries. FCPL is the largest library system in Virginia with over 12 million items in circulation and, as of 2024, 660,000 cardholders. Through their vision and mission, FCPL strives to build, inform, and engage the community through programming, technology, and educational and communal resources. Their guiding principles also promote diversity, equality, inclusion, and education through their resources and diverse community.

Users:

The target demographic for FCPL is residents of Fairfax County. They have resources for patrons of all ages and abilities. This is divided up in the “browse by audience” section of the home page into adults/50 plus, aging and disability, kids, and teens, as well as taxpayers and volunteers. This allows people to look at specific events and resources dedicated to each group.

Content:

There are 71 databases available to patrons, along with eBooks, eAudiobooks, and eMagazines. They also have a page of featured online resources that range from job search and career resources to Mango Language Learning (an online language learning service that patrons have access to using their library card). Additionally, the library has a page dedicated to helping people find their next book to read, which links to a personalized book recommendation service and the Novelist database. FCPL’s “Library of Things” page highlights the non-traditional library items patrons can loan like artwork, board games, and even light pollution monitoring kits.

Discussion of the System

Unique Features:

There are many unique features on the FCPL website that make it visually interesting. There is a slide show that rotates through images advertising for featured library events and services such as their Branch Out newsletter, a design contest for their new logo, and virtual library cards available to Fairfax County Public Schools students. If you put your mouse over one of these images, an arrow appears, allowing you to click through the advertisements at your own pace.

Another helpful feature is the virtual assistant chat that appears as a speech bubble on the right side of the screen. This chat can be in both English and Spanish, and opens with a list of frequently asked questions such as “How do I apply for a library card?” Furthermore, there is a frequently asked questions search box at the bottom of the home page, which allows patrons to search through their questions more directly.

The chat box is not the only part of the website that can be translated. In the header, there is a translate feature, which can translate the entire website into almost any language using “machine translation” and Google Translate.

Pros and Cons of the System:

Pros:

  • The website is easy to navigate, with lots of subheadings for specific information (like resources for patrons based on their age)
  • The site is also optimized for mobile use

Cons:

  • There are a lot of subsections and lists, which make it easy to find specific information, but make navigation feel overwhelming
  • Since the website is part of the Fairfax County government website, clicking “home” from a non-library page leads to the government home page
  • The search box at the top of the page searches the entire Fairfax County website, not just the library content

Suggestions for Improvement

Although the translate page feature is easily found in both the page’s header and footer, other accessibility settings such as altering font size are not available. Since there is an existing page related to disability services the library provides, I would suggest adding website accessibility services to that page as well.

Additionally, while the website highlights the library’s mission, vision, and guiding principles, the library’s history cannot be found on the website itself. It takes many clicks to find the history of individual library branches, but the system’s history is so buried on the website, I had to Google “Fairfax County Public Library history” to find a pamphlet with the information I desired. Therefore, I would suggest FCPL add the history of the library to the mission statement page and make it a more general “about us” page to provide a more well-rounded view of the library both in the past and present day.
